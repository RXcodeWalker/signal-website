And to think we actually had a chance. *Sigh*.

This game happened late at night so I couldn't stay up to watch it, and honestly, I'm glad I didn't. When I saw the 2-0 scoreline the next morning, I didn't even feel disappointed. I read a brief summary, accepted the defeat, and moved on.

That too, on the back of a massive 5-1 victory over Manchester City.

That's how accustomed I've become to these substandard performances this season. Just when it feels like we're "back in it," something pulls us right back down.

<strong>Tired Squad</strong>

I had a feeling this would happen. Going to Newcastle away just days after an intense clash against Manchester City was always going to be difficult. The squad looks exhausted from relentless back-to-back fixtures. Our players aren't superhuman; fatigue was inevitable.

This season has exposed how shallow our squad depth truly is. We lost Emile Smith Rowe, Eddie Nketiah, and Reiss Nelson in the summer for modest fees despite them being reliable bench options. Arguably, Nketiah offers similar — if not better — finishing instincts than Havertz has shown recently.

To make matters worse, we made zero signings in January despite obvious gaps in the squad. Manchester City added five players. Aston Villa strengthened wisely. Even Tottenham secured Mathys Tel on loan. Meanwhile, we stood still.

The Zubimendi signing is promising, but he arrives in the summer. We needed reinforcements immediately — a striker, a winger, perhaps another midfielder. Instead, we strengthened nothing. For a club of Arsenal's stature, that is simply not good enough.

In this match, we paid the price.

<strong>Awful Average Performance</strong>

No one truly played well, aside from Nwaneri who made a few promising runs. Raya had his first genuinely poor outing in months, making a costly error that allowed Newcastle to double their lead. It was reminiscent of the shaky mistakes he made last season, notably against Tottenham when he nearly squandered a 3-0 lead.

To be fair, Raya has been much improved this season, justifying his selection over Ramsdale. His distribution and shot-stopping have largely been solid. This simply felt like an off day.

The entire defence struggled, particularly Saliba who failed to contain Isak. The midfield lacked urgency and creativity. The front line, once again, lacked sharpness and attacking hunger.

Newcastle were superior and deserved their place in the Carabao Cup final against Liverpool. Credit to them. It will be interesting to see if they can overcome the reigning holders and secure their first major trophy since 1955.

<strong>Conclusion</strong>

This performance was disappointing across defence, midfield, and attack. Fatigue plays a role, but ultimately the responsibility falls on the board and management. The squad was not reinforced adequately in either the summer or winter transfer windows for the intensity of English football.
