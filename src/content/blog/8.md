A few days back, Arsenal drew 1-1 with Manchester United after beating PSV 7-1 in their biggest Champions League away victory in history. Many expected a dominant Arsenal win at Old Trafford. Strangely, I expected a draw.

After this season's repeated false hope, I refused to overhype a six-goal win without context. There were three reasons for that PSV demolition: PSV were out of form; their manager made serious tactical errors by spreading the midfield wide, leaving space between the lines; and Arsenal capitalised clinically on those mistakes.

Fun fact: Arsenal's xG in that 7-1 win was only 1.96. Compare that to approximately 3.50 xG across two legs against Newcastle in the Carabao Cup, where we failed to score. The difference? Clinical finishing.

Odegaard controlled that PSV game brilliantly, and even if Lewis-Skelly had been sent off, Arsenal likely still would have won because PSV refused to use a low block.

<strong>United's Low Block Problem</strong>

Manchester United did the opposite. They parked the bus. Arsenal, despite confidence from Eindhoven, lacked the attacking quality to break down a compact defence. Amorim's side were content to concede possession because Arsenal could not convert it into clear chances.

Bruno Fernandes scored a superb free-kick just before half-time. Rice equalised in the second half to salvage a point.

It was a familiar pattern: dominate possession, lack a clinical forward line, concede first, then scramble for a result.

<strong>Defensive Concerns</strong>

Raya reacted slowly to the free-kick but redeemed himself with crucial saves. United created significant chances late on, and without Raya's interventions Arsenal likely would have lost. The defence was largely stable but allowed too many dangerous moments. Against elite attackers like Vinicius, Jude, or Mbappe, such lapses could prove costly.

<strong>Title Realities</strong>

The Premier League title was effectively gone weeks ago. Now it is only a matter of when Liverpool confirm it. Arsenal's only realistic silverware chance remains the Champions League.

The 2-2 draw with PSV in the return leg was largely uneventful, aside from Sterling's two goal involvements. Unfortunately, his suspension means he misses the first leg against Madrid.

<strong>Looking Ahead to Real Madrid</strong>

Arsenal face reigning European champions Real Madrid in the quarter-finals. It will require a significant step-up in performance to progress. Interestingly, I slightly prefer facing Madrid over Atletico. Atletico's low block would suffocate our attack, whereas Madrid's more open style may allow Arsenal scoring opportunities. The real test will be our defence.

There are rumours about Saka potentially returning for the Madrid tie, but given how rusty Ben White looked upon return, I remain doubtful.

Next up is a London derby against Chelsea at the Emirates.
