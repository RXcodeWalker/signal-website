Arsenal beat Leicester City 2-0 away, securing a clean sheet and moving to within 4 points of Liverpool, who still have a game in hand. This came after devastating news of Havertz's hamstring injury ruling him out for the rest of the season. It was a massive result, and though we didn't play brilliantly, super-sub Merino scored both goals to make the difference.

<strong>Defense Should Have Been Better</strong>

As usual, we dominated possession and kept a clean sheet. However, our defence was not as solid as expected against a 19th-placed side that hadn't scored in their previous four home matches. Leicester created a few clear chances that could have changed the game. For a title-contending team facing relegation opposition, more control was needed.

<strong>Odegaard Must Step Up</strong>

Declan Rice was our best midfielder and tried to compensate for Havertz's absence with forward runs. He wasn't effective as a makeshift striker but contributed well overall. Odegaard showed flashes of creativity but must now take responsibility in front of goal, especially without Saka. Partey looked fatigued and was rightly substituted for Jorginho.

<strong>Merino Delivers</strong>

Nwaneri was electric, constantly troubling Leicester's defence. Trossard improved after shifting left, though he struggled initially as a striker. Sterling offered very little and was replaced by Merino, whose two goals prevented what felt like another frustrating draw.

<strong>Can Merino Be the False Nine?</strong>

This was only one of the first times Merino has played as a striker, making his impact even more impressive. With only three fit forwards available and Sterling out of form, options are limited. Merino's physical presence at 6ft 2in and strong aerial ability could make him effective in that role, particularly from crosses.

Trossard remains better suited to the left wing, and Nwaneri thrives on the right, where his creativity drives our attacks. Moving them centrally would reduce attacking balance. That leaves Merino as the most viable solution for now.

He displayed more confidence and clinical instinct than Havertz has shown recently, especially with his header and composed finish. While he has spent most of his career as a midfielder, this unconventional adjustment could prove beneficial.

<strong>Conclusion</strong>

I'm not entirely convinced this is the long-term answer, but it may be enough to keep us competitive in the title race. Perhaps Merino could unexpectedly become the solution we lacked up front.
