Here are six common leadership myths I learned at the Harvard YLC.

<strong>1. Leadership is Innate</strong>

Many people believe leaders are born, not made. While some individuals may possess natural traits that support leadership, effective leadership is rarely purely innate. It requires continuous learning, reflection, adaptability, and skill development. Both introverts and extroverts can become impactful leaders if they possess vision and the ability to guide others. Leadership is cultivated over time, not predetermined at birth.

<strong>2. You Can’t Be a Leader</strong>

Leadership is not reserved for CEOs or heads of state. It can exist in classrooms, families, sports teams, and community projects. Leadership begins with initiative and responsibility, not status. Anyone willing to take ownership and guide others toward a shared objective can demonstrate leadership.

<strong>3. Leadership Is About Power</strong>

Power alone does not define leadership. True leadership encompasses vision, communication, integrity, empowerment, and ethical decision-making. Influential figures such as Nelson Mandela demonstrated that empowering others often creates more sustainable impact than exerting authority. Leadership is about influence, not dominance.

<strong>4. Leaders Must Have Titles</strong>

Titles do not create leaders; leaders give meaning to titles. Many impactful individuals operate without formal recognition, yet they inspire, organize, and elevate those around them. Authority may come with a title, but leadership comes from action and credibility.

<strong>5. Leaders Control Outcomes</strong>

Leaders can shape direction and strategy, but outcomes ultimately depend on collective effort. Success is rarely the product of one individual. Effective leaders align teams, foster collaboration, and create environments where everyone contributes meaningfully.

<strong>6. Leaders Are Always Obvious</strong>

Not all leaders are visible or outspoken. Some lead quietly through consistency, support, and example. Leadership styles vary widely, making it difficult to identify leadership based solely on personality. Impact, not volume, is what truly defines a leader.
