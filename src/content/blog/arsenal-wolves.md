Well... we've bottled it again. Arsenal have made history: the FIRST-EVER time a Premier League team at the top of the table has bottled a 2-0 lead to a team in the relegation zone. We were up 2-0 against statistically the WORST team in PL history... and we found a way to draw it.

## Match Summary

Saka gets a header inside five minutes. Wolves are passive, reactive, easily second best. Martinelli misses a 1v1 again. Hincapié makes it 2–0 at the hour mark. But then Arsenal retreat, become nervy, give possession away cheaply. Wolves gain belief. Too much open space on the left allows Bueno to score a wonder strike. Really gruelling final 20 mins as Arsenal try running the clock down. 94th minute: Raya error leads to late equalizer. Final score: 2–2.

## The Real Problem: It’s Not Just the Result

Arteta's approach is to play defensive and pragmatic football. He prefers high possession, slow buildup, territorial dominance, and risk minimisation. Maybe it's based on statistics: more possession theoretically means more shots, more xG, more goals, more control of the game, less risk of conceding goals, and a higher likelihood of winning games. The first problem with this style, apart from how boring it is, is that it makes us nervy and hesitant.

When I watch Arsenal, I sometimes feel like we're scared of scoring second, third, and fourth goals. I saw it happen against United: we dominated them for 30 minutes and scored a goal. But after that, we stopped attacking. We tried protecting our lead instead of punishing them further, and this invited pressure. United got more time on the ball, created more chances, and got back into the game.

The second issue, whether Arteta likes it or not, is that the opposition love when we pull the handbrake on. Whenever Arsenal go passive, the other team pushes their backline and midfield higher, the game becomes transitional, and they start creating more chances. As a result, our supporters become anxious and Arsenal begin to hesitate. Then, the opposition scores a goal.

United enjoyed it. Wolves enjoyed it. Brentford enjoyed it. This cannot be accidental; it is a systemic problem.

## The Bigger Context: From Excitement to Fossil Football

22/23 – Naïve but Electric
Arsenal had the youngest squad in the league and we hadn't played Champions League football since 2016. There were no real expectations on our squad; in fact, many people discounted us from making it to the top five. But that season, despite losing in the end due to injuries and a lack of experience, we attacking fearlessly and we played very exciting, entertaining and dynamic football. I absolutely loved watching us play!

23/24 – Mature and Relentless
We start this season well. There's a series of poor performances in December that push us down the table, but we rebounded after the New Year. We were in superb form, absolutely ripping teams apart. 5-0 against Chelsea. 6-0 against West Ham. 5-0 against Sheffield United. The second half of our season was dominant and thrilling, though we narrowly missed out on the PL by two points.

24/25 – Pragmatic Shift
This is the season both Arsenal and Manchester City dropped off. Long-term injuries for key players like Odegaard, Saka, Gabriel, Jesus, Nwaneri, and Havertz, as well as several other recurring knocks showcased our serious lack of squad depth. We started choosing efficiency over expression, and our attack was "toothless".

Current Season – Sterile Control
We've had some big nights: 4-0 Atletico, 4-1 Spurs, 3-1 Bayern, 3-1 Inter, 4-1 Villa... And we dominated other teams with our defense at the start, going 12 games in a row without a single goal conceded. But since January? Dry. Predictable. Hesitant. Despite spending 180 MILLION GBPs on attack.

Arsenal do get a lot of criticism for playing boring football, and let me be honest here.

Boring football is fine — if it wins games. Because the truth is, you do not win the Premier League by being entertaining. You win the league by consistently winning games.

And that's precisely what we did at the start of the season. We were easily winning games 1-0 because our defense was so strong. Sure, we were boring, but we had control over the game and we won them comfortably. And I don't mind scoring for set pieces: it's just another weapon in our arsenal (pun intended). If we maintained that dominance, that consistency in winning games, the title would basically be in our hands at right now.

But this is what has happened recently:
• 2 wins in our last 7 PL games
• 10/21 points
• Dropped points vs Liverpool, Forest, United, Wolves, and Brentford

So what are we sacrificing entertainment for? We're playing boring football but what are we getting out of it? We lose control of the game, we let teams have chances, we concede goals, and we drop points. Plus, we do not score enough goals ourselves, and it's extremely frustrating to watch.

## The “It Was Just…” Excuse

I'm seeing a lot of people online making the argument that it was just one error by Zubimendi, just two wonder strikes by Dorgu and Cunha, just one banger by Bueno tonight, just one long throw-in by Brentford. These surface-level excuses about occasional bangers and defensive lapses make sense once in a while, but if this happens repeatedly, it isn't random anymore.

And let's be honest here: if any of you watched Arsenal play against Liverpool, Forest, United, Brentford, and Wolves, did you think Arsenal truly deserved to win those games?

Absolutely NOT.

In fact, Brentford deserved to beat us in the end and we were lucky to finish with a draw. The match prior, we looked very shaky against Sunderland before Gyokeres doubled our lead. And in the Carabao Cup, against Chelsea, we might have conceded a goal if not for Rosenier's defensive tactics.

## What a Title Race Should Feel Like

In 22/23: We were dreamers. I truly enjoyed watching us play.
In 23/24: We still believed. And we really were entertaining.

But this season, I just want us to win it. I don't care how we win it, if we score a set piece own goal every game from now till the end of season and nothing else, I don't care, as long as we win it.

But that's exactly my point: that's not what a title race should feel like. A title race is supposed be exhilarating, and even if we drop points, those are just the twists and turns that make it interesting. We won't win every game and it will be tough but we can still do it.

However, I don't feel that way at all. In fact, I, like many other Arsenal fans, feel like this football club owes its fans a title for the boring, heartless, soulless, gutless, passionless performances they display week in and week out, and for all the years of coming so close. It feels owed. It feels tense. It feels transactional.

Even if we somehow win the league this season, if I had the chance to rewatch the 22/23 Arsenal play fun, dynamic, entertaining, exciting football that got me off my seat almost every match, I would take that opportunity without a second thought despite the fact that we come second in the end.

That in itself should tell you all you need to know about Arsenal's season thus far.

## Where We Stand Now In The Premier League

Arsenal are still five points clear of Man City, albeit with a game in hand. However, we could have -- and should have -- easily been 13-16 points clear of them at this moment. City aren't the same team they were two years ago; they're more fragile which they showed in January when they dropped so many points. However, Arsenal didn't win their winnable fixtures either.

This was a golden opportunity to showcase our mentality, to send a statement that WE ARE WINNING THIS LEAGUE. But instead, we handed City a way back in. 10 days ago, Bernardo Silva admitted that if City lost at Anfield, the title would have been over. Today, City are effectively 2 points behind Arsenal and they're playing Arsenal at the Etihad, a fixture they're more likely to win.

The momentum is definitely with them. And for the first time in this title race, Manchester City are marginally favourites to winning the league. At the end of the day, despite all their problems, that City team knows how to win titles. Pep knows how to win titles. Arteta doesn't. This Arsenal team doesn't.

Which is why, if we slip up any further (I think we've already slipped up enough), they will smell blood. They will come for the title. They will be the ones to emerge victorious. While we crumble under pressure, they will resist. Not perfectly, not elegantly, but they will get the results, and they will win the league.

## My Final Take

I've been a massive Arteta supporter. I love how he's transformed our club from a mid-table mandem to a world-class side, one of the best in Europe, that consistently fights for the title. I am grateful to how significantly he has raised Arsenal's standards. However, the question now is whether he can get us over the line.

This season, he's had all the opportunities in the world to put us ahead of Man City and win us the title. And from one point of view, we're still five clear in the league, we're in the Carabao Cup finals, we're in the 5th round of the FA Cup, and we're the best team in the UCL. However, if the football remains conservative, the performances continue to decline, and the points aren’t coming, a serious conversation about direction becomes necessary.

Personally, if we don't win this league title, I will be ready for a new manager at Arsenal. I'll thank Arteta for building this club to the level at which he's built it, but I'll be ready to say goodbye. Despite the robust defense that he's built, quite frankly, I detest what he's done with our attack. We just spent 180 million pounds on attacking players this season.
