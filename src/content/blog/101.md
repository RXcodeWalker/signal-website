Last summer, I spent a month living at Ashoka University in Sonipat, Haryana, as part of the Lodha Genius Programme. When I first arrived, dragging my suitcase across the campus and into a dorm room that smelled faintly of detergent and unfamiliarity, it didn’t fully register what I was doing. I had never lived away from home for that long. Everything felt unfamiliar—the room, the people, even the silence at night. I still remember thinking, this is really it. I’m on my own now. For one whole month.

The programme itself was built around maths and science classes, but what stayed with me had very little to do with classrooms. What I remember most is the feeling of suddenly having complete ownership over my time. After classes ended, there was no fixed structure waiting for me. Some days, I’d go straight to the cricket nets, still in my classroom clothes, and bat until the sky turned orange and the floodlights flickered on. Other evenings, a group of us would just sit outside in the gardens, talking for hours about all sorts of things. Even with the 10 p.m. curfew and the campus boundaries, it still felt like more freedom than I had ever experienced. That's when I realized that freedom wasn't about having no limits, rather it was about having ownership over the time between them. And at Ashoka, I had a lot of ownership over my time.

At first, I did feel homesick. There were moments, especially at night, when everything slowed down and I became aware of how far away I was from everything familiar. I remember how, when I was younger, my dad used to travel frequently for work, moving between cities and hotel rooms every week. I never thought much about it then. But lying in that unfamiliar dorm room, I finally understood what that must have felt like — the quiet unfamiliarity, the need to rebuild a sense of normal in a place that wasn’t yours yet.

However, that feeling didn’t last. Without noticing exactly when it happened, the campus started to feel comfortable. The routines became mine and things felt more normal. That's when I stopped noticing how new everything was and started just living in it. And at some point, I stopped counting how many days were left till I could return home and actually started wishing there were more. 

During the first week, each day felt so long and unfamiliar, and at the end, I felt like I had been living in Ashoka for a month. But as I settled in, time seemed to speed up and the last two weeks literally flew by. This made me realize how strongly our sense of time is tied to how comfortable we feel. When everything is unfamiliar, we notice every moment, but when it becomes home, the moments blend together.

Sharing a room with someone I had never met before was part of that change. In the beginning, it felt slightly awkward — two strangers trying to coexist in the same small space. But over time, it stopped feeling like we were strangers at all. That was true for so many people there. When you eat together every day, walk to class together, play sports together, and exist in the same space constantly, something shifts. I made friends with people my age all over India, from Kashmir to Delhi to Hyderabad, and quite a few from Mumbai too.

There was also a quiet shift in how I saw myself. At home, my days had always been structured for me, with school, homework, and routines that ran almost automatically. But over there, nothing happened unless I chose it. Initially, that felt quite strange, and honestly, there was a sense of lingering emptiness. But gradually, I stopped waiting for structure and started creating it. That was when I realized independence isn’t something that suddenly appears—it grows quietly, through small decisions you make every day.

As a result, a quiet confidence started to grow within me. Small things, like deciding how to spend my time, managing my own schedule, or choosing whether to study, rest, or go out, instead of moving on autopilot between school bells and routines in Mumbai, gave me the experience of structuring my own life. And more importantly, I realized I liked that responsibility and control.

Of course, the academic side was interesting. We explored ideas like investing, cryptography, microscopy, and how architecture shapes emotion in films. The classes were different from school—they felt more open, more driven by curiosity than by exams. But when I think back, the academics feel almost secondary to everything else I experienced outside the classroom. What stays with me most are the unplanned moments. Sitting on the grass after sunset, watching the campus slowly empty. Walking back to the dorms late at night, when everything was quiet except for the sound of footsteps and distant conversations. Playing sports until my legs hurt.

At some point, without realizing it, the campus stopped feeling temporary. My dorm room felt normal. The walk to the dining hall felt routine. The people around me felt like part of my everyday life. It was so strange to think that just weeks earlier, all of it had felt unfamiliar.

Overall, I realized I wasn’t someone who needed familiarity to feel comfortable. I could build that comfort anywhere. I also saw that independence wasn’t something to fear but rather something I naturally grew into. By the end, LGP felt less like a temporary programme and more like a glimpse into an independent future.

Eventually, when I left, I wasn’t the same person who had arrived with that suitcase a month earlier. The campus that once felt unfamiliar had become a place I didn’t want to leave. For the first time, I had experienced what it meant to live independently — not just physically, but mentally. I returned home with more confidence, stronger friendships, and the realization that I was ready for independence and excited for it.

When I arrived, I remember that anxious thought... I’m on my own now.
When I left, that thought no longer felt intimidating. It felt natural.
