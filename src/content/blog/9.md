To all my amazing football-loving readers,

The most predictable injury in the Premier League has finally happened.

Kai Havertz will miss the rest of the season due to a hamstring injury sustained during training in Dubai. Ironically, a warm-weather camp intended to boost fitness ended the season of our only senior centre-forward.

<strong>Injury Crisis Deepens</strong>

This adds to Jesus's ACL injury suffered in the FA Cup defeat to Manchester United. Saka is out until at least April, potentially longer. Martinelli is sidelined for nearly a month. In total, four senior forwards are injured, three long-term.

We were already struggling for goals even when Havertz and Martinelli were fit. Now the likely forward line consists of Nwaneri, Trossard, and Merino as a makeshift striker. Sterling offers little impact, and depth is practically nonexistent.

<strong>A Preventable Situation</strong>

To some extent, this is misfortune. But it was entirely foreseeable. Arsenal entered January needing at least one striker, arguably two, even before further injuries struck. Yet we made zero signings.

This came after humiliating exits from domestic competitions and dropped points in crucial matches. Despite clear warning signs, the board chose inaction.

<strong>January Window Failure</strong>

Loan options such as Kolo Muani, Asensio, and Rashford were available. Depth could have been strengthened without major financial strain. Even recalling loaned players like Fabio Vieira would have added cover. Instead, the club gambled on internal solutions.

Havertz played virtually every match, pressing relentlessly as a false nine following a demanding summer tournament. An injury felt inevitable. Now, just after the window closed, it has arrived.

<strong>Accountability</strong>

Arteta initially resisted external signings, preferring internal options, but later acknowledged the need for reinforcements. While he shares some responsibility, the greater blame lies with the board and management.

The lack of urgency suggests satisfaction with Champions League qualification rather than genuine ambition for major trophies.

<strong>Conclusion</strong>

This January window may ultimately define our season. The damage is done. The best we can hope for now is that Merino adapts successfully or that the club corrects course in the summer.

If similar mistakes persist, deeper structural changes may be necessary.
